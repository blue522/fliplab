Why Fliplab? FlipLab models best teaching practice

FlipLab is about using technology to make the classroom more interactive and relevant.

Khan Academy has sparked interest in “flip” their classroom, but much of this work has focused on the lower rungs of Bloom's Taxonomy: memorization, understanding and application. As “flipped classroom” interest increases, there is growing recognition that critical thinking skills, collaboration, and project-based learning are more important than ever.

The problem: we're still waiting for a comprehensive model of teaching that blends these two opportunities.

That's why there is FlipLab - an opportunity for educators to actualize the best of these parallel teaching trends. FlipLab gathers educators to explore and share best teaching practice while leveraging recorded content. Innovative teachers serve as mentors through the process of discovery.

What makes FlipLab different?

FlipLab is a 21st century professional development experience. The participants collaborate to create a roadmap for a great flipped classroom. Everything developed at FlipLab is shared with other teachers under a Creative Commons license.

You will leave a FlipLab workshop with flipped lessons tailored for your classroom and ready to implement. You'll pilot them in your classroom and work with peer coaches to debrief the results. Even better, you'll be part of a network of educators pushing the boundaries of educational best practice.

Build better classroom experiences.

  • Learn from teachers around the country who are already flipping their classes
  • Connect with peer teachers in your subject area
  • Leave with new, ready-to-use lessons tailored to your classroom
  • Draft the roadmap for what a great flipped classroom looks like
  • Gain firsthand experience with a range of technology and tools
  • Get support from peers and FlipLab mentors as you put it in practice

What is a “flipped classroom”?* defined

At its core, a flipped classroom (also called flipped teaching, flipped instruction, vodcasting, educational video-on-demand) is a format for removing some of the lecture-based lessons from classrooms and giving students the ability to learn that content in their own time at their own pace. This is done through recording video-based lectures and posting them online for students to engage and respond to.

There are several advantages to this model of teaching:

  • Flipped teaching means that an educator doesn't need to guess at what speed to deliver content – with students watching lectures at home they can move at their own speed and review concepts as necessary.
  • Without large portions of classroom time spent lecturing, educators can use that time to see students working through projects and assignments that would have previously been done in isolation at home: break out sessions can occur spontaneously, students can work in mentor-based groupings, jigsaw opportunities, supplemental support, etc.

Jon Bergmann, Jerry Overmyer and Brett Wilie outline some other benefits in somewhat of a flip model manifesto, published at The Daily Riff:

  • A means to INCREASE interaction and personalized contact time between students and teachers.
  • An environment where students take responsibility for their own learning.
  • A blending of direct instruction with constructivist learning.
  • A classroom where students who are absent due to illness or extra-curricular activities such as athletics or field-trips, don't get left behind.
  • A class where content is permanently archived for review or remediation.

How is a flipped classroom different from other educational movements?

Flipped classroom isn't the first time that technology has been held up as a solution for the worldwide challenges that are occurring in education, and it won't be the last. However, the focus of flipped teaching is different from other examples in that the technology itself is simply a tool for flexible communication that allows educators to differentiate instruction to meet individual student needsand spend more time in the classroom focused on collaboration and higher-order thinking. The technology solutions are varied and don't rely on a single vendor to implement. Flipped teaching is a great example of using technology with intention.

All participating teachers will leave with hands on experience utilizing flipped classroom technology and at least one complete lesson plan – including a recorded segment and an in-class follow-up – ready to use in their classroom. Furthermore, all content will be shared via Creative Commons license with the broader educational community.

Pre-workshop:

The 2-day workshop will follow the ‘flipped' model. Participants will gain a framework of understanding prior to arrival; an overview of project-based learning, 21st century skills, differentiated learning and flipped instruction. They will also be introduced to tools that can be used to record and distribute media.

Day 1:

With their subject-matter team, participants will identify core standards that are well suited for video lecture and gain hands-on experience with video and lecture capture technology.

Day 2:

Subject-matter teams will will develop lesson plans that build on this foundational knowledge using best teaching practice. Lesson plan emphasis will be on project-based learning, group discovery and differentiated instruction.

Post-workshop:

Each subject-matter team will be subdivided into peer coach pairs for ongoing support after the initial workshop. In the moth following FlipLab, teachers head back to their classrooms and the real world of daily teaching. In this period, FlipLab leaders will visit local attendee classrooms and observe lessons in action. Peer coaches will also be invited to attend, as possible. Debriefs and self-evaluations will help teachers learn from what works, and what does not.

Final reflection:

6 weeks after the FlipLab workshop, teachers will come back together (either face-to-face or via video conference) as a cohort to share their experiences, infer best practices, and publish initial findings.
